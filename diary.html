THE PROBLEM
Looking for muses on Instagram

Nearly three or four years ago, I've created an Instagram account to get inspiration from people who have been learning to code and sharing their journey. Every day, they were writing what they're doing. It made me inspired to see them trying to learn something.

When I created an Instagram account, it wasn't easy to find people who share their design or programming journey. THE DISCOVERY
Let the adventure -research- begin

After the idea hit me, I tried to reach people who have been sharing their journey through digital channels to understand:

  • Why did they decide to share this learning process on social media?
  • What are their reasons for using Instagram?

I conducted interviews with 5 people and asked these questions:

What motivates you to keep sharing all the process?
Having a lot of followers is important for you or it doesn't matter?
What do you like about Instagram?
If you have two different projects, do you have a different account for each project?

Also interviewed with another 3 people who follow these learning stories to find out:

  • What are the reasons behind following the learning process of other people?

Asked these questions:

How did you find these people?
Does it motivate you to follow these accounts?
Do you research a lot to find new people?
Do you share your opinions with people you follow?
What do you like about Instagram?
The findings

This research uncovered something interesting for me. Unlike my foresight, people's first aim was tracking the process instead of getting more followers. I wasn't expecting that.

After when they answered what are their reasons to share, I asked them to rank their answers according to its importance. Based on all the people's results, "tracking the process" took the first place.

The following answers are their top 3 reasons for what motivates them to keep sharing all the process.

  • Tracking the process
  • Getting inspiration
  • Gaining recognition
Getting into the core

To make sure I was building the right thing for users, I needed to focus on the sufficient features that would satisfy people. I focused on what exactly the core functionality of the app should be and the problems it should solve. Digging deeper into the answers of people, I decided to specify features under two main titles.

Share the process

  • Different workspaces/projects
  • Add stories

Get inspiration

  • Discover
  • Follow
THE VISUAL OUTPUT
Focus on date not popularity

I sorted stories by date because I thought of the project as an online diary. I didn't put any other choice on the table.

Also, I put all the stories under two main titles:

  • Design
  • Coding

While doing this, I aimed to filter stories according to their content and thus making it easy for users. (In fact, in the first version of the project, I'd divided stories into two categories; designers and developers. But after realizing that these two titles cause confusion, I changed it to design and programming.)

A new story is on the way

We all know that the primary purpose of this project is to share stories, so I wanted this option to be easily accessible when users want to add a new story. And I decided to put it in the sidebar as a navigation link. (not like a small button on the home page)

Ready for meeting new people?

Sometimes, finding people who share his/her learning process requires research and it takes time in that applications don't recommend them directly.

Therefore I added the "Discover" page to the project, which will make it easier to find different people and projects. And on this page, there're two categories; designers and developers.

The more space the more usability

To prevent the complexity that may occur from having different accounts for each project, I thought that users should have more than one project space to share all their projects under a single account instead of many accounts. While doing that, my goal was to provide users more useful and simpler experience.
